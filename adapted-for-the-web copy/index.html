The Stranger

The boxes arrive on a Monday morning. No one pays attention to them except me.

“Unclaimed property,” the slip says. Another life folded into cardboard.

I open the first box expecting junk — old receipts, bills, notebooks no one will read.

But inside, I find a stack of journals. Some neat. Some chaotic. And one that feels… different.

Pages torn. Words frantic. Drawings like maps of a mind fighting itself.

I flip through it slowly.

Circles.

Colors.

Symbols painted in layers.

And a face. A young man. Something about him feels familiar, but I can’t place it.

I take the journal home that night. It feels wrong to leave it in the fluorescent quiet of the library. The entries drift between clarity and confusion:

“I forget the street again.”

“Blue means safe.”

“I need to remember tomorrow.”

“This is slipping.”

The next day, I follow one of her sketches. A street corner, a narrow wall that once held a mural — now painted over. But if I look closely, the outline of a symbol still ghosts through the paint.

I start seeing her everywhere.

On old brick.

On peeling doors.

On concrete pillars at the underpass.

Her marks aren’t messages to the world. They’re messages to herself. Anchors she left to stay steady.

Back at the library, I study the portrait again.

This time, the familiarity hits me with force.

It’s me.

My face, years younger, drawn by someone I never met.

I close the journal slowly. The room feels too small.

Why me? Why my face?

At the very beginning, before the forgetting, there was color everywhere.

Paint under my nails.

Sketches drying in the sun.

A small apartment filled with light.

A life that felt intact.

A mind that felt whole.

A woman who never imagined she would one day lose track of her own days, her own walls, her own name.

Before that, the forgetting began quietly.

Misplaced keys.

Missed appointments.

Words dissolving mid-sentence.

I wrote everything down.

Colors.

Directions.

Names of streets.

Symbols to guide me back to myself.

My journals were not art.

They were survival.

Earlier still — a day when the sun was soft and the world less frightening — I sat on a bench trying to slow my breathing. People walked past me, faces blurring like water.

Then one face didn’t blur. A young man looked at me for half a second with quiet, ordinary kindness. My mind latched onto him. A landmark. Something real when everything else wavered.

I opened my journal and drew his face as fast as I could. Not because he mattered — but because I needed something to hold onto.

Before everything fell apart, I painted to stay steady.

Little shapes on walls,

corners,

stones,

curb edges.

Not to be seen. Not to be admired. But to remind myself: You were here. You are still here.

They send me somewhere else the next day. A bed. A room. A counselor asking gentle questions. I try to remember my address. The name of the street. The color of the door. Nothing returns. I do not go back. Not because I choose to — but because the memory is gone.

At the hospital, the white walls hum. I answer questions I don’t understand. I try to explain what I was trying to hold onto. They take the journal from me “for safekeeping.” I nod. Minutes later, I forget it exists.

The lights blur as I step into the store. Voices swirl around me. Someone asks if I’m alright. I can’t find the words. Everything slips. The floor feels unsteady. I hear someone say, “Call someone. She needs help.”

Night. Cold air, empty streets. I run without knowing why. My breath breaks. My hands shake. The journal presses against my chest like something alive. If I let go, I will disappear.
